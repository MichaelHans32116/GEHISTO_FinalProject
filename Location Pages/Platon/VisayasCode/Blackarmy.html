Ruperto Kangleon
March 27, 1890 – February 27, 1958

The Black Army emerged as one of the most important guerrilla groups in the Visayas region, particularly in the islands of Leyte and Samar. The group was led by Ruperto Kangleon, a former officer of the Philippine Constabulary, who later became a respected figure in the post-war Philippine government.

Under Kangleon’s leadership, the Black Army organized local resistance fighters into a coordinated force that carried out sabotage missions, gathered vital intelligence, and launched guerrilla attacks against Japanese troops. Their operations disrupted Japanese movements and weakened enemy control over the region.

Black Army

The Return of Douglas MacArthur
October 20, 1944

The Black Army played a key role in supporting the U.S. military, especially in the months leading up to General Douglas MacArthur’s return to the Philippines. Their efforts helped prepare Leyte for the historic Leyte Gulf landings in October 1944, which marked the beginning of the liberation of the Philippines from Japanese occupation. The bravery and discipline of the Black Army not only contributed to military success but also symbolized the Filipino people's determination to fight for freedom, sovereignty, and national dignity.
