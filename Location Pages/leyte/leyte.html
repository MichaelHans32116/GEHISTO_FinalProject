MACARTHUR'S RETURN

The Battle of Leyte Gulf - October 1944

Prelude to Battle

After being forced to leave the Philippines in 1942 with his famous promise "I shall return," General Douglas MacArthur spent two years planning his comeback. The Battle of Leyte Gulf was the largest naval battle of World War II and paved the way for MacArthur's dramatic return to Philippine soil.

The Naval Battle

Fought from October 23-26, 1944, the Battle of Leyte Gulf was the largest naval battle in history. It involved nearly 300 ships and marked the first use of organized kamikaze attacks by Japanese forces.

The Landing at Leyte

On October 20, 1944, MacArthur waded ashore at Leyte to fulfill his promise. His famous speech "People of the Philippines, I have returned" marked a turning point in the Pacific War.

Aftermath

The Allied victory at Leyte Gulf effectively destroyed what remained of Japanese naval power. It secured the beachhead for the liberation of the Philippines and cut off Japan from its occupied territories in Southeast Asia.

Timeline of Events

March 1942
General Douglas MacArthur was ordered to evacuate the Philippines as Japanese forces advanced, famously vowing "I shall return" before departing by PT boat for Australia where he assumed command of Allied forces in the Southwest Pacific.
Oct 17, 1944
U.S. Army Rangers made the first Allied landing in the Philippines since 1942 by capturing Suluan Island, securing the eastern entrance to Leyte Gulf and allowing minesweepers to clear paths for the main invasion force.
Oct 20, 1944
The U.S. Navy landed four Sixth Army divisions ashore on Leyte on October 20, 1944. Japanese aerial counter-attacks damage USS Sangamon (CVE-26) and a few other ships, but do not hinder the landings. Later in the day, General Douglas A. MacArthur, USA, gave his "I have returned" radio message to the Philippine people.
Oct 23-26, 1944
Japanese units attacked U.S. forces and shipping on 23 October. On 24 October, they launched multiple air raids: morning, afternoon, and night attacks with bombers. The U.S. deployed 36 fighters (plus 16 ready) - 28 for attack forces, 8 for beach patrol. That day, 150-200 Japanese planes (mostly bombers) struck Leyte. U.S. forces shot down 66 (18 probable) using 50 planes, losing 3. Most U.S. aircraft targeted the Japanese fleet, not air defense.
Dec 1944
On 25 December, when elements of the 77th Division had seized Palompon, the last important port on Leyte, General MacArthur declared that all organized resistance had ended. He said in a message to General Krueger: 'Heartiest congratulations on capture of Palompon. This closes a campaign that has had few counterparts in the utter destruction of the enemy's forces with a maximum conservation of our own. It has been a magnificent performance on the part of all concerned.'
